About

Generalist by instinct, researcher by training.

I like work that lets me move between ideas, people, methods, and practical delivery. I’m happiest on projects where I can coordinate moving parts, engage with different groups, analyse complex information, and help turn research into something open, transparent, and genuinely useful.

Approach

Range is the point.

My current PhD focuses on individual differences in adolescent additional-language learning, but the broader thread across my work is psychological research that connects evidence, lived experience, and real-world practice. I’m interested in research that is genuinely usable, collaborative, and meaningful beyond academia.

Current work

What I’m working on now

My doctoral work sits within psychology, learning, and research methods, while drawing on my earlier experience in digital mental health, large-scale trials, patient and public involvement (PPI), and study coordination.

PhD review

Evidence synthesis and meta-analysis

I am conducting a systematic review and meta-analysis of predictors of adolescent additional-language learning, bringing together evidence from psychology, education, linguistics, and cognitive neuroscience.

PPI and engagement

Adolescent Advisory Group

I am running an advisory group with adolescents to help ensure my research is informed by the people it is intended to understand and support.

Applied research

Pilot adaptive learning programme

I am supporting the development of a pilot adaptive language-learning programme, with an interest in how psychological evidence can inform tools that are practical, engaging, and personalised.

Research interests

Rather than one narrow topic, I work across a few connected themes.

My work clusters around questions about how people learn, how psychological research is delivered, how lived experience shapes better research, and how evidence becomes useful.

Learning, language, and development

Individual differences, adolescence, additional-language learning, educational experience, and cognitive and environmental predictors of learning outcomes.

Mental health and digital interventions

Psychosis research, remote cognitive remediation, digital mental health tools, relapse prediction, intervention acceptability, and participant-centred research.

Evidence synthesis and research methods

Systematic reviews, meta-analysis, mixed methods, qualitative interviewing, quantitative analysis, transparent workflows, and making complex evidence digestible.

PPI, lived experience, and useful research

Research shaped with people and communities, not just about them: advisory groups, accessible materials, creative dissemination, and meaningful involvement.
